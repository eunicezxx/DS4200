From Budget to Box Office: Factors in Film Financial Success

Stephanie Chen, Yanjie Du, and Xinxiang Zhang

Introduction

The film industry is incredibly complex: box office revenue, production costs, or audience reception may make or break a film. Since we are all passionate about films, we wanted to investigate a movie-related dataset. Our collective interest led us to want to explore the relationship between production budgets, box office revenue, and audience reception. This topic is interesting because movie success can be defined by many different factors, some of which may not necessarily occur together. Additionally, movies are often huge investments, so movie success is incredibly important. We can ask many interesting questions, such as: does domestic success guarantee global success? Do audience ratings predict box office performance? Because the factors that impact box office success can be so fragile, and often must justify the investment of production budgets, we thought this would be an interesting relationship to explore. We became interested in the relationship between production money invested in the film, box office, and critical reception (audience ratings). Our project task is to understand these relationships through a data-driven approach, exploring a dataset of film information to identify the differences in financial return and critical reception of films as a function of their production budget. We can use our findings to help filmmakers, investors, and analysts determine whether future films will succeed.

Data

Our project will use a dataset of movie statistics from Kaggle titled The Ultimate Film Statistics Dataset - for ML. The dataset sources include Wikipedia, The Numbers, and IMDb. The size of the dataset is 4241 rows and 14 columns. We can download the data as a .csv file.

The dataset provides comprehensive statistics related to the film, with each row representing a movie. First, it includes general information about each movie: the movie titles, production dates, genres, runtime minutes, and director information. Then, it includes information about the audience reception of the movie, such as the average ratings, number of votes, and approval index. Finally, it provides data related to the financial investment and success of the movie: production budgets, domestic gross earnings, and worldwide gross earnings.

Specifically, we will be focused on a few features: the movie titles, average ratings, production budgets, domestic gross earnings, and worldwide gross earnings. The movie titles are quite self-explanatory: unique strings that are the title for each movie. The average ratings are the average rating given by online users for a particular movie. This context is important because it means that the ratings may exclude populations that do not post reviews online, such as older or very young age groups. The production budgets are the amount of money used in USD to produce the movie. The domestic gross earnings are the revenue in USD earned from tickets sold in the movie’s home/domestic market. The worldwide gross earnings are the revenue earned from global ticket sales in USD. These characteristics will allow us to analyze the financial and audience popularity of films in different budget categories, making it suitable for our analysis.

Given the structure of the data set, we will pre-process the data to ensure consistency and accuracy. We will clean up missing or incorrect values, such as negative values. We also break down audience scores into different categories and divide movies into three budget categories to allow for further analysis. Using the data, we can spot trends and anomalies in the film industry.

Takeaway

The interactive scatter plot above shows the relationship between movie budgets and their domestic and worldwide gross, with each point representing a movie. Color is used to distinguish between gross types, where blue dots represent worldwide gross and the red dots represent domestic gross. The x-axis is the production budget, and the y-axis is the gross amount. The gridlines of the graph allow for easier identification of budget and gross amounts.

Overall, there is a positive correlation between gross and budget. However, we can also see that there is a lot of variation in the points given the same budget, so budget is not the only factor that is important for movie success. Worldwide gross also tends to be greater than domestic gross, which is expected. In particular, outliers like Avengers: Endgame and Avatar: The Way of Water have extremely high worldwide gross amounts, showing the potential of targeting worldwide/international audiences. The highest grossing films, as expected, have the highest budgets, but most films tend to cost below 300 million dollars and make below 1.5 billion dollars in revenue, with only a few outliers surpassing these numbers. We can see that both lines (with domestic in red and worldwide in blue) have a positive slope, indicating a positive correlation, where higher budgets tend to be associated with higher gross amounts for both worldwide and domestic gross. Interestingly, the blue line, representing worldwide gross, has a steeper slope than the red line, representing domestic gross. Therefore, the main takeaway is that movie producers may want to target international audiences, as a higher budget tends to mean more gains worldwide than domestically.

This grouped bar chart illustrates the average domestic and worldwide box office revenues for movies grouped by their rounded audience rating (from 1 to 10). From the chart, we observe that movies with higher audience ratings generally tend to earn more both domestically and worldwide. The average revenue rises significantly for films with ratings between 7 and 9. This suggests that positively rated movies are more likely to succeed commercially. There is also a noticeable gap between domestic and worldwide gross in higher rating levels, indicating a strong global appeal for well-rated films.

The main task of this graph is to explore the relationship between average ratings and revenue, and whether this is different domestically versus globally.

The main takeaway is the positive relationship between ratings and average gross. Both domestic and worldwide gross tend to be higher when ratings are higher, which is expected, as better movies should attract more viewers. The average worldwide gross is also always much higher than the domestic gross, which reinforces what we saw in previous graphs: that appealing to an international audience can be very financially beneficial.

The main takeaway from this visualization is that runtime does not appear to be associated with domestic or worldwide revenue. Summary

Our data visualizations’ main task was to explore the relationship between the production budget, audience ratings, and box office performances of movies, with a focus on what produced the highest domestic and worldwide gross amounts. We used line charts, scatter plots, a bar chart, box plots, and a histogram/bubble chart to explore the film dataset and to understand how these factors affect each other in complicated ways.

The line chart comparing budgets and gross revenues showed that films with higher budgets do tend to have higher gross amounts, with the worldwide gross being much higher than the domestic gross. This might suggest that appealing to global, rather than domestic, audiences is financially beneficial. However, we also saw in later graphs that there is a lot of variability, and budget is not the only factor. Films with the same budget experience a lot of variability in terms of their gross. Films with moderate budgets have dense representation, which may suggest that this is a good balance between market appeal and managing investments/risk.

Later graphs introduced audience rating as an interesting factor. They demonstrated that a higher audience rating does suggest higher revenue. The bubble chart showed that the films that earn the most money overall have both a high budget and very high ratings. We also saw that the movie quality actually remains quite similar across rating categories, suggesting budget may affect revenue in a different way other than quality.

Finally, the last two graphs investigate runtime and genres as possible factors in movie financial success. While we found that runtime does not appear to be associated with revenue, genres do differ significantly in revenue, with those appealing to broad audiences (Animation, Adventure, and Sci-Fi) grossing the highest on average.

A more interesting further exploration would be the audiences of movies. Do movies that appeal to particular demographics of people gross the highest? We have seen how genres influence revenue, so understanding what demographics different genres target could be an interesting addition to understanding what affects film financial success. Additionally, we can ask: how have movies changed over time? Do the patterns between revenue and budget change over time, as movie effects and editing become more advanced and as audiences change? These would be very interesting further opportunities for exploration.

Overall, our analysis looked at the relationship between production budgets, box office performance, runtime, genres, and ratings. We looked at takeaways across many different kinds of charts in order to understand how these factors come together to potentially influence a movie's revenue, sometimes in surprising ways.

The line chart that compared budgets and revenues showed that films with higher budgets do tend to earn more. This is especially true for global revenue, which we found to be consistently higher than domestic revenue. However, there is a lot of volatility, where films across all kinds of budgets still differ a lot in revenue, particularly for high budget films. Some break box office records, while others severely underperform, despite high investmnet. We saw that films with moderate budgets are most common, so this range might be a good balance between investment and market appeal.

The bubble chart also helped us look at audience ratings and relate them to budgets and revenues. High audience approval is not only for high budget films, and there are films of all budgets present in all ratings. We also saw this in our box plot, where audience ratings were similar across all budgets. Clearly, creativity, storytelling, and quality do not depend solely on budget, though the vast majority of films earn ratings between 4-8. However, across all ratings, there is a consistent trend: a higher budget does mean higher revenue. Thus, to achieve quality, a high budget is not needed, but to achieve extreme financial success, a high budget and a high rating are both necessary.

Overall, these visualizations together demonstrate how maximizing movie revenue is extremely complex and challenging. While we saw that higher budgets tend to mean higher worldwide and domestic grosses, there is still a lot of variability in revenue across all budgets. Furthermore, we found that across all budgets, audience ratings tend to remain similar. Therefore, higher budgets do not guarantee good movies, despite better ratings also being associated with higher revenue. We found that the genres Animation, Adventure, and Sci-Fi are most financially successful, appealing to broad audiences. Thus, the best films, which achieve the most impactful financial success, require large production budgets, excellent audience ratings, and broad audience appeal. While these factors seem intuitive, it is also important that we looked to the data to confirm or reject our hypotheses rather than assuming they are true. After establishing these baseline factors, we can further delve into the data: do certain audiences spend more on movies? How have these trends changed over time? Do critic scores matter more than audience scores? Why are some films such significant outliers in revenue? There are many interesting and complex factors we can now further explore.
